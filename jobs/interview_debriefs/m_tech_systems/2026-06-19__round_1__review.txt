1. Walk me through an implementation you owned.
Answer:
One strong example is my implementation work with Aptian products, where I supported the full lifecycle from discovery through post-go-live support. My role was to gather and validate requirements, align stakeholders across teams, coordinate with technical resources on configuration, and own testing and validation before release.
A big part of the job was making sure the scope was clear early so we didn't run into avoidable issues later. I worked closely with business users and technical teams to make sure the solution matched actual operational needs, not just a surface-level request. After configuration, I supported UAT, data checks, and post-go-live stabilization.
What's most relevant about that experience is that it required both technical understanding and client-facing communication, which is what I understand this role needs as well.
Follow-up: What made that implementation challenging?
Answer:
The hardest part was usually stakeholder alignment. Different teams often wanted different things, so the challenge was turning those competing priorities into one realistic plan. That's where I added value - getting clarity early, reducing ambiguity, and keeping the implementation practical.

2. What experience do you have with UAT and data validation?
Answer:
I've been directly responsible for both. In my previous work, I coordinated testing by validating workflows, checking whether data populated correctly, and confirming outputs matched expectations before anything went live.
I also worked across time zones with development teams, so testing required structure and coordination. I'd run scenarios, document issues, verify fixes, and then walk internal users through what changed so they could validate the business side as well.
I see UAT as more than testing functionality - it's really about building trust that the system will perform correctly in a live environment.
Follow-up: How do you know when something is ready for go-live?
Answer:
For me, it comes down to three things: the requirements are met, the data is behaving correctly, and the end users understand the change well enough to work in the updated process. If one of those is missing, it's usually a sign more validation is needed.

3. Tell me about a customization or upgrade you managed.
Answer:
At East West, the ERP was already in place, so a lot of my work focused on customizations, upgrades, and service-pack-level changes. In those situations, I would define the business need, document functional requirements, and work with the vendor or services team to assess feasibility, scope, and timeline.
One thing I paid close attention to was downstream risk. A requested change might solve one problem but create another if it affected existing customizations or required a broader upgrade. So I always looked at the request in the context of the full environment, not just the immediate ask.
After that, I coordinated testing, validated the results, and reviewed the changes with users before go-live. My role was really to connect business needs with technical execution while managing risk.
Follow-up: What's your approach when a requested customization may not be feasible?
Answer:
I try to be transparent early. I want to understand what the business is truly trying to achieve, because sometimes the exact request isn't feasible, but the underlying need can still be solved another way. I think that consultative mindset is important in implementation work.

4. How do you handle competing stakeholder priorities?
Answer:
That's a normal part of implementation work. My approach is to clarify what each group actually needs, identify where the conflicts are, and then work toward a realistic solution that supports the business without creating unnecessary complexity.
I've worked with finance, operations, and technical teams, and each group tends to frame requirements differently. So I spend time early in discovery making sure expectations are explicit, because if that doesn't happen, scope creep usually shows up later.
I think one of my strengths is bringing structure to those conversations and helping teams align around what is required versus what is just preferred.
Follow-up: What if they still disagree?
Answer:
Then I try to bring the discussion back to business impact, feasibility, and project scope. If needed, I'd document options and tradeoffs clearly so decision-makers can choose with the right context instead of everyone talking past each other.

Answer:
The industry is new, but the implementation discipline is not. My background is in complex ERP-style environments with multiple modules, cross-functional stakeholders, testing, reporting, and process-heavy systems. So while I would need to learn the poultry side of the business, the core implementation work is already familiar to me.
I also understand that in a role like this, learning the business language matters just as much as learning the software. My approach would be to ramp quickly by listening carefully, asking good questions, and grounding myself in how the client actually operates.
Follow-up: What do you think would be hardest for you early on?
Answer:
Probably the industry terminology and some of the business context behind it. But I think that's learnable. The stronger foundation I already bring is implementation process, ERP logic, stakeholder management, and systems thinking.

This role also feels like a strong fit because the product is ERP-like in structure, which is familiar to me, but specialized enough that there's a real learning curve. I find that appealing because it gives me a chance to apply what I already know while continuing to grow.
Follow-up: Why leave your previous environment for this one?

I'm looking for a role where implementation is a core focus and where I can bring the experience I've built in ERP systems into a team-based consulting environment. This opportunity feels aligned with both my background and the kind of work I want to keep doing.

3. What experience do you have with UAT and data validation?
Answer:
That's one of the areas where I've had a lot of direct involvement. I've coordinated UAT, run test scenarios, validated outputs, and checked whether the data was pulling and displaying correctly before releases went live.
For me, that work is not just "does the screen function?" It's also "is the system behaving the way the business expects?" and "can the user trust the result?" So I'd usually look at both the workflow and the data behind it.
I've also worked with offshore development teams, so part of that process was coordinating testing across time zones, identifying issues quickly, retesting fixes, and then walking internal users through what changed so they could validate it from their side too.
Skeptical follow-up:
"How technical are you really here?"
Stronger answer:
I'd say I'm strong from a systems and functional standpoint. I'm comfortable validating logic, understanding data flow, running queries or checks where needed, and working closely with technical teams. Pressure-test follow-up:
"What's the biggest mistake people make in UAT?"
Answer:
Treating it like a formality. If people just click through a script without understanding what success is supposed to look like, they miss the real business issues. I think good UAT has to validate both functionality and business reality.

4. Tell me about a customization or upgrade you managed.

claim ownership too broadly if the role was shared
Do this instead:
answer in the first sentence
be precise
separate vendor-side implementation from client-side enhancement work
sound calm, not rushed
